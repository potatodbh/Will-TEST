What a will is (and isn’t)

Built around Ontario’s plain-language guidance, with classroom-friendly explanations.

What is a will?

A will is a document that explains where your property and possessions go after you die. It can also appoint a person to care for minor children.

  • Property, money, and personal items
  • Executor (estate trustee)
  • Guardians/custodians for minors

When does it take effect?

A will takes effect only after the person dies. The executor has no legal power while the person is alive, even if they become incapable.

If you need someone to manage property while you’re alive, that’s a Power of Attorney.

Why people get into trouble

Most disasters come from unclear wording, invalid witnessing, or losing the original will. When there’s no will, Ontario’s intestacy rules decide.

  • Common-law partners don’t automatically inherit
  • Courts may appoint an administrator
  • Guardianship decisions can shift to the court

Validity essentials (fast rules)

Plain language version of the execution rules you must follow.

Formal will (most common)

  • In writing
  • Signed at the end
  • Signed/acknowledged in front of two witnesses present at the same time
  • Witnesses sign in the testator’s presence
Practical tip: avoid using beneficiaries as witnesses. Keep it clean to reduce challenges.

Holograph will (handwritten exception)

  • Wholly in your own handwriting
  • Signed by you
  • No witnesses required (if it truly meets holograph rules)
Holograph wills can be easier to challenge if wording is unclear. Use caution.

Accuracy notes (Ontario)

We keep the language simple, but we don’t oversimplify the important parts.

Marriage and wills

Ontario changed the rules so marriage does not automatically revoke a will for marriages on/after January 1, 2022. If your will is old, you should still review it after major life changes.

Intestacy and spouses

If you die without a will, distribution follows Ontario intestacy rules. Common-law partners do not automatically inherit under intestacy rules (marriage matters).

Quick cautions

  • Store the original will where the executor can access it.
  • If the original can’t be found, courts may presume it was revoked.
  • Update your will after major life changes (new child, new property, separation).
